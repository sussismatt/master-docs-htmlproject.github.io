Taxi Tipping: A Breakdown by Neighborhood

How tips for Chicago cab drivers vary by community area and over time

By Matt Sussis

If the rise of ride-sharing services such as Lyft and Uber and the depreciating value of their medallions weren’t enough, Chicago taxi drivers have also received lower than average tips, according to analysis of city data.

The data, provided by the city’s Department of Business Affairs and Consumer Protection, showed that the average tip per ride for taxi drivers during the first full work week in May this year was 19.7 percent, or $2.95, for customers paying with credit cards, compared to 20.7 percent, or $3.11, in March 2014.

Much has changed in the taxi industry since 2014, and tipping behavior has changed, too. Chicago taxi medallions – permits that allow Chicago taxi drivers to pick up customers – have plunged in value from over $320,000 in 2014 to below $80,000 today, according to the city of Chicago. Medallion values have plunged as riders, especially younger riders, have shifted toward ride-sharing apps like Uber and Lyft and demand for taxis has shrunk.

It seems that the remaining yellow cab riders, despite their apparent loyalty to yellow cabs in an era of ride-sharing, may be less generous to their drivers than the larger pool of riders in the past had been.

However, tipping behavior did vary greatly by location.

Some areas donated 40 percent on average between the two years analyzed, and other areas averaging only 10 percent. Among the most generous of Chicago’s 77 community areas were Edison Park, Ashburn, and Jefferson Park, all of which tipped at least 30 percent on average when paying by card.

Some cab drivers said they noticed the sharp variations in behavior based on pick-up and drop-off location.

“Certain areas have people who are very generous, some not at all,” said Chicago cab driver Khalilur Howlader. “But if someone wants a cab, I can’t choose and I can just hope they tip me.”

Chicago’s most generous community areas were located on the North and West Sides of the city, which tend to have higher median household incomes than the South Side. Of the ten highest tipping communities, none were located on the South Side of the city (although some were southwest of the city center, such as Ashburn and Clearing). This was the case in both years analyzed.

Tipping does appear to be strongly correlated with income. The five highest earning areas all tipped at least 20 percent and the lowest five all tipped no more than 20 percent. However, there were outliers within income levels—East Garfield Park tipped over 25 percent despite a median household income below $25,000, and Calumet Heights tipped only 10 percent despite a median household income above $55,000.

Some of the previously stingier districts did become more generous, and vice versa. In Forest Glen, tips averaged over 27 percent in 2014 but fell to roughly half of that in 2017. Conversely, McKinley Park tips nearly doubled, from 16 percent in 2014 to over 30 percent this year. Most neighborhoods, however, had relatively consistent tipping habits. Nearly 60 percent of community areas had an average tip that fluctuated by less than 5 percent between 2014 and 2017.

Notably, the analysis only looked at tips made by credit card, as tips were not recorded in cash transactions. When paying with credit card in Chicago, taxi pay screens generally give three pre-selected options to users—15 percent, 20 percent or 25 percent along with an “other” option. While many riders tipped 20% or less, a small number of riders paid well over 20% which skewed the average higher—especially on low-fare rides (e.g. tipping $2 on a $3.00 ride). Many of the cash transactions were for lower fare rides, so it is possible that tips made with cash could have skewed the average higher, although of course the data is not available.

The shortest rides generally led to the highest-percentage tips

In New York City, cab drivers lobbied to have the default tip options for credit cards raised to 20 percent, 25 percent or 30 percent in order to encourage more generous tips. While Chicago drivers would likely see higher tips under such a system, they have not yet lobbied the city for a similar change.

While the percent tipped was consistent over time, the number of rides had fallen sharply. In the period analyzed, the number of rides declined 43 percent, distributed fairly evenly across community areas. In some areas such as Lincoln Park and Lake View, rides declined well over 50 percent. In Lincoln Park, there were 1,151 in the 2014 period and only 553 in the 2017 period. In Lake View, the number of rides fell from 1,215 to 553.

The one community area in which the number of rides actually increased was Loop, perhaps as the availability and proximity of cabs in the city center made hailing a cab easier than waiting for an Uber or Lyft.

The city’s Department of Business Affairs and Consumer Protection has taken steps to mitigate the impact of declining rides on the livelihoods of cab drivers. It has increased taxi fares by 15 percent up to $2.25/mile, reduced card transaction fees, and slashed the tax on transferring medallions by 80 percent. None of these steps, however, have been able to curb the continual decrease in yellow cab usage.

The city also introduced the CURB app as the official app for Chicago taxis, a ride hailing app for taxis that connects users to the closest available taxis, in order to help them compete with the likes of Lyft and Uber.

“At CURB, we talk about a four-point strategy for an app: it must be easy, available, reliable and safe,” said CURB co-founder Sanders Partee. “If we quickly don’t provide the most available rides to people, rides are going to continue to go down and we won’t remain competitive.”

While CURB, combined with the steps taken by the Consumer Affairs bureau, might help alleviate some of the pain, drivers’ income ultimately remain dependent on two factors: the number of rides they provide per day, and the average tip per ride. As long as both taxi usage and tipping behavior are declining, drivers will continue to struggle.

“For most of us, tips are what pay the bills,” said Howlader. “Less rides mean less tips.”
